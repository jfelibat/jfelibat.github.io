Trusting the Process
Braeden Cheverie-Leonard, student face of esports on campus, has a seat at the table as SU develops a major and varsity teams around gaming.

By Jesús Feliciano-Batista

Published : 22nd December 2023

Photo from Cheverie-Leonard's Instagram

Braeden Cheverie-Leonard can’t legally buy beer or go to a bar with his friends. He also can’t buy cigarettes or cannabis. Braeden can’t even rent a car in most states, at least not for a few more years. He is, after all, just 19 years old.

None of that, though, has stopped him from helping a Power Five school develop a new varsity team and academic program.

Syracuse University sophomore Braeden Cheverie-Leonard is the face of esports at the school. Ask a student with a passion for the field and they will send you his way, as will professors at the sports management program. Cheverie-Leonard has even been asked to join faculty during interviews for people filling roles related to esports at Syracuse, said Director of Sports Management Michael Veley.

After joining Syracuse last year, Cheverie-Leonard has taken on an important role as student-voice in the process of developing Syracuse’s varsity esports program, as well as the new Esports Communications and Management degree that the school will launch next fall.

“I like to say that I’m a sports management major, and my minor is developing the esports program,” Cheverie-Leonard joked, he doesn’t really have time for an actual minor at the moment.

“I think I am the voice,” Cheverie-Leonard said of his role in Syracuse esports. “Joey's (Gawrysiak) the one who can actually get things done, but he kind of lets me rant, and gets things done.”

Cheverie-Leonard, captain of the Call of Duty club team at Syracuse, sees himself as the biggest reason why the school will start competing at esports tournaments in more than just a club level. There was not much of a structure before he got to the school, much less any staff to develop the structure.

When Cheverie-Leonard arrived, the Call of Duty club team was not even practicing and had not played a game in three years. He helped revive the team by recruiting players and entering them into competitions. The first season was “tough,” but he kept pushing to draw more attention to the team and turn it from club to varsity.

Still, his freshman year in Syracuse was not easy. Despite hating to hear the word “no,” it followed him everywhere. The process of building up the team was full of negations.

On the club level, Cheverie-Leonard was proactive in trying to use his experience to better develop the teams. He approached last year’s president with ideas for tournaments and budget uses that were rejected.

It was difficult to get things done and timetables were constantly changing. He also hated the idea of calling the Call of Duty team a club team, as he considered the team’s schedule to be as competitive and intensive as varsity-level sports. Syracuse policy makes the team wear shirts that say “club” on them.

The team’s captain taped over the word. To this day, Cheverie-Leonard uses the shirt with the tape.

His frustration even led him to consider transferring elsewhere. Cheverie-Leonard said this was never a real option, but he felt limited by the whole process Syracuse was implementing and that the school was not doing enough to develop the program.

“I appreciate all the money and time we're putting into building this, but the longer we wait, the less time I have left,” Cheverie-Leonard said. “Every day that passes, other schools are catching up on developing their programs, and we need to be the first, we need to be the best.”

This changed during his sophomore year, and Cheverie-Leonard believes it will continue to change as the program comes together and positions are filled.

“I think the results are going to come easier now that we are actually establishing a program. I feel like there's going to be a lot less obstacles now that we actually have staff members and it's not just some student wanting to do this,” he said.

This year, Syracuse hired Joey Gawrysiak as executive director of esports. This was more than just a sign of support from the university, but a new voice with the power to actually change things and move the process forward.

Cheverie-Leonard was asked to join the call where Gawrysiak interviewed for the position. The school wanted student input, and he was a clear choice for Veley.

Veley first got to know Cheverie-Leonard during a class trip to Los Angeles as part of a course – “Sport Management LA Immersion.” Here they began talking, and two elements stood out about the student: his passion for esports and personality, said Veley.

“He’s locked in. This is his passion, this is what he wants to do. He generates his own stimulation and wants to learn. Because of that, he can walk into a room and carry on a conversation with a company president before the age of 20. He's not intimated, but he’s not cocky or arrogant,” Veley said. “He lights up the room.”

Gawrysiak was Cheverie-Leonard's preference to fill the position, and the two have already collaborated in developing the program. This semester, Gawrysiak requested the student’s opinion on the search for a new competitive director, an important person who will oversee the competitive teams and recruitment.

Gawrysiak asked Cheverie-Leonard to his office to discuss some aspects regarding logistics and facilities. At the end of the meeting, the executive director gave the student a batch of ten résumés and asked him to rank them. They compared candidates and notes until they agreed on the better picks.

Cheverie-Leonard’s No. 1 choice was Gawrysiak’s third. The candidate is now the favorite for the position.

“It's really humbling, and really exciting. Everybody says I play a big role in this, and I do, but to actually see: ‘hey, come into my office, I want to run something by you,’ and then to get a pile… It felt really rewarding,” said Cheverie-Leonard. “I think I looked in the mirror and laughed. It was like, ‘this is really happening?’ It's really cool.”

Gawrysiak also keeps Cheverie-Leonard up to date regarding the academic side of the program.

“Joey and I try to pencil in an hour meeting every week, but we probably text cumulatively over an hour a day. Whether that's: ‘hey, this is the physical construction update with facilities,’ or ‘I just got this position approved,’ or ’I just drafted up this course. What are your thoughts?’ That's a daily thing,” Cheverie-Leonard said.

Cheverie-Leonard’s role in the academic side is smaller than his role with the team, but he has no problem with that. Despite his past frustrations and fatigue in his work, the sophomore still considers this a passion.

“I'm very passionate about getting my team to a point to compete for a national championship, which is a very difficult and long road ahead of us. But I think we can do it,” Cheverie-Leonard said.

“When he gets passionate about something, he puts his efforts into it…That passion and that drive has helped him,” said Kris Leonard, Braeden’s father. “It’s what has pushed him, and he's had some success that has really validated his ability.”

His role in developing the program continues to evolve as the process itself evolves. While he recognizes he can’t do much in terms of action, he also knows his influence as the face for esports on campus.

Cheverie-Leonard met for a one-on-one breakfast and tour with a prospective competitive director three weeks back. The candidate is his No. 1 option for the role, and, if hired, the esports club team will officially become a varsity team. The sophomore will also make a presentation in front of the university’s board of trustees regarding esports and the varsity team.

“There was only one piece of Syracuse missing, and it was the esports program; he wished they had a program at the varsity level, and now he's working towards creating that,” said Janelle Cheverie, Braeden’s mother. “So, he took the college that matched 99% of what he was looking for, and added that 1%. He has worked hard at doing so and being a pivotal part in that.”

Still, Braeden is just a sophomore. He has priorities he must get to; it's not just meetings with the people in charge of developing the program.

Cheverie-Leonard believes he is at a crossroads in his life and career. As a sports management major, his career goal is to be the general manager of a team, or a coach, or maybe even an owner. He recently took an interest in sales as well and is considering this as a future career. He’s not even sure if esports are in his future.

His parents do not mind. It was a risk to pick Syracuse when other schools offered him more money, but they were proud of their son for following his passion. Whatever move he decides to make, their pride and support won't change.

“The biggest thing that I've tried to instill in him is to make a difference, and to find a passion. He has done both of those,” said Janelle Cheverie.

“He's definitely got the skills to make it work, and the passion that he really wants to push through on it. So, I'm proud as hell of him,” said Kris Leonard. Leonard highlighted his son’s responsibility and commitment as well.

Braeden takes his role as captain seriously. He tries to be the first one in the esports room before practices and the last one to leave, while making sure his team is up to the task ahead.

The team captain holds and leads practices that can reach eight hours in length, only to then keep tabs on his teammates about habits and practicing outside of their scheduled meetings. On days where the team meets for less than five hours, he logs into the game and checks up on the time each player puts in. You have to play at least five hours per day to be a starter on the team.

To make sure the varsity team is ready to go next semester, he needs to make sure the team practices and understands the newest Call of Duty. There will be pressure to win in the first year and justify the program. It's all part of the process.

Braden arrives at the esports room in the Barnes Center. He sets up his station and boots up the most recent Call of Duty game. The team starts practice and, halfway through, one of his teammates complains about his hangover.

The teammate stands up, stretches and starts doing jumping jacks.

“Looks like you had fun,” Braeden says, he has already informed the team they will be practicing all weekend, so there will be no time for drinking or hangovers.

“There’s nothing to do this weekend, anyway,” his teammate says.

“There’s CoD to do this weekend,” Braeden replied, before putting his headset on and resuming practice.
